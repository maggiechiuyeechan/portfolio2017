The Project

A City of Homelands was my cumulative term project for my final year at OCADU. It was an interactive documentary that combined physical computing, data visualization and ethnographic media to tell a transmedia narrative about the personal and cultural story of immigration, identity and its relationship to the politics of space in Toronto.

The project can be considered an experimental work that straddles the categories of future cinema, data visualization, interactive installation and graphic design. It explores different methods of experiencing and interfacing with documentary media and data.

There are 3 main components to the installation. The first is a “database” documentary in the form of 15 non-linear video clips. The second are photo cards with an image, and information of a video clip on each card, embedded in each card is a RFID tag. The third is a RFID station that appears to be a photo frame and a knob; hidden underneath the surface of the photo frame is an RFID reader. Once someone inserts a photocard into the photoframe, the associated video clip starts to play.

RFID embedded photo cards trigger a video when inserted into the photo frame

The content of the documentary consists of multiple interviews, observational videos, photography and texts. Each video clip, in different ways, unpacks the notions of “space” and “place” in relationship to the Chinese Canadian Diaspora, they explore the interactive making of the multiple Chinatowns sprawled across Toronto and the formation of ethnic identities, both collective and individual. To investigate Toronto’s immigrant geography, this project draws theoretical inspiration from non-representational theory, affect studies and politics of everyday life. These theoretical influences provided the conceptual scaffolding for the content of the documentary.

The 15 video clips are organized through the logic of a database (based off Lev Manovich’s concept of ‘database cinema’ in his article “Database as Symbolic Form”*). The intrigue in using a non-linear, ‘database’ narrative relies on its ability to to capture our modern experience of multiplicity. This structure enables multiple narrative levels to operate simultaneously. A City of Homelands concurrently tells the story of an individual immigrant, the story of a place, the story of a community and the analysis social processes by cultural theorists. The photo cards supplement the experience of the database narrative by providing tertiary information about the documentary, and encouraging the audience to performatively ‘search’ and ‘parse’ the collection of images in order to explore different narrative segments. In this way, the audience traverses the database, following its links. Thus, the interactive documentary can be understood as the experience of multiple trajectories within this database.
